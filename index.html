Blueberry Pancakes
Blueberry Pancakes

Deluxe Blueberry
Pancakes

Yield 12 pancakes · serves 4
Total time ~45 minutes
America's Test Kitchen · Andrea Geary
Section 1
Mise en Place — Ingredients & Tools
Ingredients
5 tbspunsalted butter (divided)
490 gbuttermilk
1large egg
213 gblueberries
all-purpose flour
malted milk powder
sugar
baking powder
baking soda
table salt
vegetable oil
Tools
1medium mixing bowl
1Pyrex quart measuring cup
whisk & rubber spatula
⅓-cup dry measuring cup or #16 scoop
kitchen scale (recommended)
12-inch nonstick skillet
thin, wide spatula (for flipping)
paper towels
rimmed baking sheet + wire rack (optional)
Section 2
Prep
1
Melt & cool butter. Melt 3 tbsp butter and set aside to cool slightly. Cut the remaining 2 tbsp butter into ½-tbsp pieces; set aside for cooking.
2
Mix dry ingredients. In a medium bowl, whisk together 283 g (2 cups) all-purpose flour, 3 tbsp malted milk powder, 2 tbsp sugar, 2 tsp baking powder, ½ tsp baking soda, and ½ tsp salt.
3
Mix wet ingredients. In the Pyrex measuring cup, whisk together 490 g buttermilk, 1 large egg, and the 3 tbsp melted butter. Butter clumps are fine.
4
Combine. Make a well in the dry ingredients, pour in the wet mixture, and whisk until just combined — a few lumps should remain. Do not overmix. Fold in 213 g (1½ cups) blueberries.
5
Warm oven (optional). If serving all at once, set a wire rack on a rimmed baking sheet and heat oven to 200°F to keep finished pancakes warm.
Section 3
Cook
1
Season the skillet. Heat a small splash of vegetable oil in the skillet over medium-low until shimmering. Wipe out with paper towels, leaving just a thin film.
2
Test the heat. Drop 1 tbsp batter into the center. After 1 minute it should be pale golden brown. Adjust heat up or down as needed, then discard this test pancake.
3
Cook in batches of 3. Melt ½ tbsp butter in the skillet and spread with a spatula. Once sizzling, scoop three ⅓-cup portions of batter and gently spread each into a ~4½-inch round. Cook until edges are set and first side is deep golden brown, 3 minutes.
4
Flip. Flip each pancake with a thin spatula and cook 1 minute. Slide pancakes around the pan to collect butter, then cook until the second side is deep golden brown, 2 minutes more.
5
Repeat. Serve immediately or transfer to the warm oven. Repeat with remaining ½ tbsp butter per batch for 3 more batches. Serve with salted butter and maple syrup.
America's Test Kitchen · Deluxe Blueberry Pancakes · Andrea Geary, 2022

Oven-Roasted Tomato Sauce
Oven-Roasted Tomato Sauce

Oven-Roasted
Tomato Sauce

Yield about 2½ cups
Total time ~2½ hours
Ad Hoc at Home · Thomas Keller
Section 1
Mise en Place — Ingredients & Tools
Ingredients
228–32 oz cans San Marzano whole peeled tomatoes
1 cupyellow onion
1 cupleeks (white and pale green parts only)
1 cupfennel
garlic
light brown sugar
red wine vinegar
canola oil
kosher salt
freshly ground black pepper
1sachet (see below)
To Make the Sachet
1bay leaf
3thyme sprigs
10black peppercorns
1garlic clove, smashed and peeled
cheesecloth (7-inch square)
kitchen twine
Tools
cutting board & knife
large ovenproof Dutch oven or baking dish
food processor
colander or strainer
wooden spoon or spatula
covered storage container
Section 2
Prep
1
Preheat oven to 350°F.
2
Make the sachet. Lay out a 7-inch square of cheesecloth. Place the bay leaf, 3 thyme sprigs, 10 peppercorns, and 1 smashed garlic clove near the bottom edge. Fold the bottom up and over them, roll once, tuck in the two side edges, and continue rolling. Tie both ends securely with kitchen twine. Set aside.
3
Chop aromatics. Finely chop 1 cup yellow onion. Mince 1 cup leeks (white and pale green parts only). Finely chop 1 cup fennel. Mince 1 tbsp plus 1 tsp garlic.
4
Assemble Dutch oven. Combine 2 tbsp canola oil, the onion, leeks, fennel, and garlic in the Dutch oven. Sprinkle generously with kosher salt.
5
Prep tomatoes. Drain both cans of tomatoes and remove the seeds. Coarsely chop half the tomatoes. Puree the other half in a food processor. Keep chopped and pureed tomatoes separate until ready to add to the pot.
Section 3
Cook
1
Roast aromatics. Place the Dutch oven uncovered in the oven and cook for 45 minutes to 1 hour, until the vegetables are tender and beginning to caramelize.
2
Add sugar and vinegar. Stir in 2 tbsp light brown sugar and 2 tbsp red wine vinegar. Return to the oven for 20 minutes, until the liquid is absorbed. Remove from oven.
3
Combine and roast. Add the chopped and pureed tomatoes to the vegetables along with the sachet. Season with salt and pepper to taste. Return to the oven for 1½ hours, stirring every 30 minutes, until the sauce is thick and full-flavored. To test: run the side of a spoon through the sauce — if it runs back together immediately, it needs more time.
4
Finish. Discard the sachet. Let the sauce cool to room temperature. Refrigerate in a covered container for up to 1 week.
Ad Hoc at Home · Thomas Keller · Oven-Roasted Tomato Sauce · Makes about 2½ cups
Perfect Chocolate Chip Cookies
Perfect Chocolate Chip Cookies

Perfect Chocolate
Chip Cookies

Yield 16 cookies
Total time 1 hour + 20 min cooling
America's Test Kitchen · Charles Kelsey
Section 1
Mise en Place — Ingredients & Tools
Ingredients
14 tbspunsalted butter (divided)
1large egg
1large egg yolk
149 gpacked dark brown sugar
99 ggranulated sugar
248 gunbleached all-purpose flour
300 gsemisweet chocolate chips or chunks
85 gchopped pecans or walnuts, toasted (optional)
baking soda
table salt
vanilla extract
Tools
2large baking sheets (18 × 12-inch)
parchment paper
110-inch skillet (not nonstick)
1large heatproof bowl
1medium bowl
whisk
heatproof spatula or wooden spoon
#24 cookie scoop or 3-tbsp measure
kitchen scale (recommended)
wire rack
Section 2
Prep
1
Preheat oven to 350°F convection or 375°F conventional. Adjust rack to middle position. Line 2 large baking sheets with parchment paper.
2
Mix dry ingredients. Whisk together 248 g all-purpose flour and ½ tsp baking soda in a medium bowl. Set aside.
3
Toast nuts (optional). If using, toast 85 g chopped pecans or walnuts in a dry skillet over medium heat until fragrant, 3–5 minutes. Set aside to cool.
Section 3
Cook
1
Brown the butter. Melt 10 tbsp butter in a 10-inch skillet (not nonstick) over medium-high heat, about 2 minutes. Continue cooking, swirling the pan constantly, until butter is dark golden brown and smells nutty, 1–3 minutes. Remove from heat immediately.
2
Melt remaining butter. Transfer browned butter to a large heatproof bowl. Stir in the remaining 4 tbsp butter until completely melted.
3
Add sugars and seasoning. Whisk in 99 g granulated sugar, 149 g dark brown sugar, 1 tsp salt, and 2 tsp vanilla extract until fully incorporated.
4
Add eggs. Whisk in 1 large egg and 1 large egg yolk until smooth with no sugar lumps, about 30 seconds. Let stand 3 minutes, then whisk 30 seconds. Repeat resting and whisking 2 more times until mixture is thick, smooth, and shiny.
5
Add flour and mix-ins. Stir in flour mixture with a spatula until just combined, about 1 minute. Stir in 300 g chocolate chips and nuts if using. Give dough a final stir to ensure no flour pockets remain.
6
Portion dough. Divide into 16 portions (~3 tbsp each, or use a #24 scoop). Arrange on prepared baking sheets, 8 per sheet, spaced 2 inches apart.
7
Bake. Bake one sheet at a time until cookies are golden brown and still puffy, edges set but centers still soft, 10–14 minutes, rotating the sheet halfway through. Transfer to wire rack and cool completely before serving.
America's Test Kitchen · Perfect Chocolate Chip Cookies · Charles Kelsey, 2013
Caramelized Onions

Caramelized
Onions

Cook time  50 minutes
Temp  275°F / 135°C
Control Freak · Medium Intensity
Section 1
Mise en Place — Ingredients & Tools
Ingredients
1000 gyellow onion, thinly sliced
100 gunsalted butter
10 gkosher salt
Tools
Control Freak induction burner
wide pan with lid
cutting board & knife or mandoline
wooden spoon or spatula
kitchen scale
timer
Section 2
Prep
1
Slice onions. Thinly slice 1000 g yellow onion. Uniform thickness ensures even caramelization.
2
Set up burner. Set the Control Freak to 275°F / 135°C, Medium Intensity.
Section 3
Cook
1
Melt butter. Place the pan on the burner. Once it reaches temperature, melt 100 g butter.
2
Add onions. Add the sliced onions and season with 10 g kosher salt. Stir to coat thoroughly in the butter.
3
Cook covered. Cover with a lid and cook for 50 minutes, stirring every 10 minutes to ensure even caramelization.
4
Finish. After 50 minutes the onions should be deeply caramelized and jammy. Cool and reserve.

Breaded Chicken
Fingers

Yield  25–30 pieces · serves 4
Fry temp  350°F
America's Test Kitchen
Section 1
Mise en Place — Ingredients & Tools
Ingredients
1 lbboneless, skinless chicken breasts
2large eggs

Preheat oven to 350°F.

3
Slice potatoes. Peel the potatoes. Cut a thin lengthwise slice off one side of each potato so it rests flat on the mandoline. Toss the slices in the cream periodically to keep them coated and prevent oxidizing.
4
Prepare pan. Brush a 10 × 6½ × 3-inch pan with half the softened butter. Section 2
Prep — Day Before
1
Soak raisins. Combine 100 g black raisins and 150 g boiling water in an airtight container. Soak for 2 hours. Drain any excess water and set aside.
2
Mix dough. In the Ooni mixer bowl, dissolve 9 g SAF Red yeast in 305 g water. Add 625 g bread flour, 25 g sugar, 17 g vegetable oil, 12 g malt syrup, and 13 g fine salt. Mix on low speed with the hook until homogeneous, 5 minutes. Increase to medium speed and mix to full gluten development, 4–6 minutes. Dough should be stiff and smooth. Perform the windowpane test to confirm full gluten development.
3
Add raisins and cinnamon. Turn mixer off. Add the soaked raisins and 10 g cinnamon. Mix on low speed until just incorporated — do not overmix.
4
Bulk ferment. Transfer dough to a lightly oiled tub or bowl and cover well with plastic wrap or a lid. Ferment at room temperature until a small piece of dough dropped in a glass of cold water floats within 10 seconds — approximately 1–1.5 hours with SAF Red at 9g, though cinnamon's yeast inhibition means it may take longer. This is the only point in the process where the float test is used — once divided and shaped, the dough is degassed by handling and will not float regardless of fermentation state. No folds; keep covered throughout.
5
Divide. Turn dough onto a clean worktable. Divide into 8 pieces of ~140 g each. Keep dough covered as you work — it dries out easily.
6
Preshape. Roll each piece into a tight round ball. Cover and rest for 20 minutes.
7
Shape bagels. Shape each ball into a bagel ring. Place on sheet pans lined with parchment paper dusted with cornmeal. Do not cover — the exposed surface will form a skin that helps create a brittle crust.
8
Cold proof. Refrigerate uncovered at 4°C / 39°F for 12–16 hours.
Section 3
Bake — Day Of
1
Preheat oven and steel. Place baking steel on the middle shelf. Preheat convection oven to 220°C / 425°F. Allow at least 45–60 minutes for the steel to fully saturate with heat.
2
Prepare lye solution. Wearing gloves and protective eyewear, add 6 g lye to 4 kg water in a large non-aluminum pot. Stir to dissolve. Bring to a gentle boil — not a rolling boil.
3
Boil bagels straight from the refrigerator. 4
Bake. Slide the sheet pan onto the baking steel. Bake at 220°C / 425°F convection until deep golden brown, 12 minutes. The boiling drives the final expansion and sets the crust; the lye gives a shiny, slightly brittle finish.
5

Mom's Chocolate Cake

Mom's Chocolate Cake

Yield serves 10
Total time ~3 hours
Family recipe
Section 1
Mise en Place — Ingredients & Tools
Cake
113 gunsalted butter (1 stick), room temperature
2large eggs, separated, room temperature
365 gbuttermilk, room temperature
57 gunsweetened chocolate (2 oz)
227 gcake flour, sifted
298 gsugar
2 tspbaking soda
½ tspsalt
2 tspvanilla extract
Frosting
2egg whites (reserved from cake)
298 gsugar
76 gcold water
¼ tspcream of tartar
⅛ tspsalt
1½ tspvanilla extract
Tools
19 × 13-inch cake pan, lightly greased
1stand mixer
1hand mixer
1double boiler
1medium bowl (dry ingredients)
1medium heatproof bowl (frosting)
2small bowls (egg yolks · egg whites)
sifter or fine mesh strainer
spatula · toothpick
instant-read thermometer
kitchen scale
Section 2
Prep
1
Bring to room temperature. Remove 113 g butter, 2 eggs, and 365 g buttermilk from the refrigerator and let come to room temperature, 30–60 minutes. Ideal butter temperature is 65–68°F — pliable and soft but still cool to the touch.
2
Preheat oven to 350°F conventional or 325°F convection.
3
Melt chocolate. Melt 57 g unsweetened chocolate in a double boiler over low heat. Set aside to cool slightly.
4
Sift dry ingredients. Sift and whisk together: 227 g cake flour, 2 tsp baking soda, and ½ tsp salt.
5
Separate eggs. Yolks in one small bowl, whites in another. Refrigerate whites until needed for frosting.
6
Prep frosting bowl. In a medium heatproof bowl combine 298 g sugar, ¼ tsp cream of tartar, and ⅛ tsp salt. Set aside.
Section 3
Make Cake
1
Cream butter. Fit the stand mixer with the paddle attachment. Beat 113 g butter on medium for 1 minute until creamy. Butter must be at 65–68°F.
2
Add sugar. Add 298 g sugar and beat on high for 3 minutes until light, pale, and fluffy. The full 3 minutes is essential — this builds the air structure that makes the cake rise.
3
Add yolks. Add the egg yolks one at a time, beating on medium-high for 30 seconds after each.
4
Add chocolate and vanilla. Add the melted chocolate and 2 tsp vanilla. Mix on medium for 10 seconds.
5
Alternate flour and buttermilk. On low, add in this order: ⅓ flour → ½ buttermilk → ⅓ flour → ½ buttermilk → ⅓ flour. Mix until just combined after each addition. Always start and end with flour.
6
Pan and bake. Pour batter into the greased pan, making the center slightly lower than the edges. Bake 35–40 minutes. Do not open oven before 30 minutes.
7
Check doneness. The cake smells done, batter has pulled away from the sides, a toothpick in the center comes out clean, and the center springs back when pressed.
8
Cool. Cool in the pan for at least 1 hour before frosting.
Section 4
Make Frosting and Frost
Use the hand mixer for all frosting steps — it lets you hold the bowl over the double boiler while beating.
1
Prepare double boiler. Bring water to a boil in the bottom, then reduce to a steady simmer.
2
Add egg whites and water. Add the 2 reserved egg whites and 76 g cold water to the frosting bowl. Bowl and beaters must be scrupulously clean and dry.
3
Beat off heat for about 1 minute.
4
Beat over double boiler on high for 7 minutes until frosting is thick, glossy, and holds stiff peaks. Keep water at a steady simmer — not a rolling boil.
5
Finish frosting. Remove from heat, add 1½ tsp vanilla, and beat for 1 minute more.
6
Frost the cake. Spread frosting over the cooled cake immediately — it sets quickly. Cake must be fully cool before frosting.

Risotto with
Tomatoes and Basil

Yield serves 4–6
Total time ~1 hour
Modernist Cuisine · adapted
Section 1
Mise en Place — Ingredients & Tools
Risotto
375 gcarnaroli risotto rice
600 gchicken stock
575 gwater (combined with stock to make light stock)
~500 gadditional light chicken stock (for adjusting consistency)
180 gsauvignon blanc or other fruity white wine
150 gParmigiano Reggiano, grated (food processor)
300 gtiny tomatoes (Sparkle or cherry), rinsed
50 gshallots, finely chopped
50 gcanola oil
1large bunch fresh basil
½ tspsalt (plus more to taste)
Tools
1pressure cooker
1food processor (for cheese)
1small saucepan (reserve stock)
1large bowl or measuring vessel
1strainer (for tomatoes)
4ingredient bowls
spatula · ladle · kitchen scale
instant-read thermometer
Section 2
Prep
1
Make light stock. Combine 600 g chicken stock and 575 g water in a large vessel. Add 180 g wine. Add 2–3 tsp salt if stock is unsalted. Set aside — no need to warm.
2
Warm reserve stock. Warm ~500 g additional light stock (300 g stock + 290 g water) over low heat and keep at a simmer — for adjusting consistency at the end.
3
Grate cheese. Grate 150 g Parmigiano Reggiano in a food processor. Transfer to an ingredient bowl.
4
Stage remaining bowls. Set out bowls for 375 g rice and ½ tsp salt. Rinse 300 g tomatoes and leave in the strainer.
5
Prep basil. Wash basil but do not cut — chiffonade just before adding at the end.
6
Prep pressure cooker. Finely chop 50 g shallots and add directly to the pressure cooker. Add 50 g canola oil and ½ tsp salt.
Section 3
Pressure Cook
1
Sauté shallots. Heat pressure cooker over medium-high, stirring, until shallots are soft and translucent, about 3 minutes. Do not allow them to color.
2
Toast rice. Add 375 g rice and cook, stirring, for 1 minute.
3
Add liquids. Add the stock and wine mixture. Reduce heat to medium-low.
4
Pressure cook. Seal and bring to 1 bar / 2 lines pressure. Cook for exactly 6 minutes, starting the timer only when full pressure is reached.
5
Release pressure. Let decrease naturally if time allows. For the last bit of pressure, run tepid water over the rim of the cooker.
6
Check rice and add cheese. Rice should be al dente. Let cool until temperature drops to 140–160°F / 60–70°C before adding cheese — above 170°F causes proteins to seize. Add Parmigiano gradually in 3–4 additions, stirring constantly after each until creamy and homogeneous.
7
Finish. Adjust salt. Add the tomatoes — they soften gently from residual heat. Chiffonade the basil and fold in. Adjust consistency with warm stock as needed. Serve immediately.
Pressure reference: Hissing = 1.2 bar. Second red line = 0.8 bar. First red line = 0.4 bar. Target is 1 bar — between the two red lines.

Modernist Cinnamon Raisin Bagel

Modernist Cinnamon
Raisin Bagel

Yield 7 bagels · 140 g each
MC prep 12 hours ahead
Cold proof 12–16 hours
Bake 220°C / 425°F convection · 10–12 min
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 5 · adapted
⚠ Lye Safety. Lye is caustic and can severely burn skin and eyes. Always wear gloves and protective eyewear during any step involving lye. Never use aluminum cookware with lye. Keep children away.
Section 1

Lye Solution
4 kgwater
6 glye (food-grade sodium hydroxide)
Dough Relaxer Options
0.0625 gAdolph's meat tenderizer — easiest to find
~6 dropsfresh pineapple juice — widely available (0.3125 g)

0.01%fresh kiwi juice (liquid — add after full gluten dev.)
0.01%papain, powdered (dry — add with dry ingredients)
0.03%fresh papaya juice (liquid — add after full gluten dev.)
Adolph's and pineapple juice amounts are based on 625 g flour. Percentages for other options are also based on flour weight. Dry relaxers add with dry ingredients; liquid relaxers add after full gluten development.
Tools
1immersion blender (for MC solution)
1Ooni mixer
1lightly oiled tub or bowl (for bulk ferment)
2sheet pans lined with parchment, dusted with cornmeal

1baking steel (preheat in oven)

gloves & protective eyewear (for lye)

plastic wrap
Section 2
Prep — Day Before
1
Make methylcellulose solution. Combine 325 g water and 3.25 g methylcellulose powder (F50) in a container. Mix thoroughly with an immersion blender until fully combined with no clumps. Allow to hydrate at room temperature for at least 12 hours. The solution will thicken as it hydrates. Making slightly more than needed accounts for what sticks to the container — use exactly 308 g when mixing the dough and discard the rest.
2
Soak raisins. Combine 100 g black raisins and 150 g boiling water in an airtight container. Soak for 2 hours. Drain any excess water and set aside. Raisins add density to the dough and along with the cinnamon will slow fermentation — expect bulk ferment to take 1–1.5 hours or longer.
Section 3
Prep — Once MC Solution Is Ready
1
Mix dough. In the Ooni mixer bowl, dissolve 9 g SAF Red yeast in the 308 g methylcellulose solution. Add 625 g bread flour, 25 g sugar, 17 g vegetable oil, 12 g malt syrup, 13 g fine salt, and 6.25 g polydextrose. If using Adolph's meat tenderizer, add 0.0625 g now; or if using fresh pineapple juice, add ~6 drops now. Mix on low speed with the hook until homogeneous, 5 minutes. Increase to medium speed and mix to full gluten development, 4–6 minutes. Confirm full gluten development with the windowpane test. Turn mixer off. Add the soaked raisins and 10 g cinnamon. Mix on low speed until just incorporated — do not overmix.
2
Bulk ferment. Transfer dough to a lightly oiled tub or bowl and cover well with plastic wrap or a lid. Ferment at room temperature until a small piece of dough dropped in a glass of cold water floats within 10 seconds — approximately 1–1.5 hours or longer. Cinnamon inhibits yeast activity and the raisins add density, so this batch may take longer than a plain bagel — be patient and rely on the float test, not the clock. This is the only point where the float test is used — once the dough is divided and shaped it is degassed by handling and will not float regardless of fermentation state. No folds; keep covered throughout.
3
Divide. Turn dough onto a clean worktable. Divide into 7 pieces of 140 g each. Keep dough covered as you work — it dries out easily due to low hydration.
4
Preshape. Roll each piece into a tight round ball. Cover and rest for 20 minutes.
5
Shape bagels. Shape each ball into a bagel ring. The relaxer makes extension easier — if the dough springs back, rest it a few minutes longer before continuing. Place on sheet pans lined with parchment dusted with cornmeal. Do not cover — the exposed surface forms a skin that helps create a brittle crust.
6
Cold proof. Refrigerate uncovered at 4°C / 39°F for 12–16 hours.
Section 4
Bake — Day Of
1
Preheat oven and steel. Place baking steel on the middle shelf. Preheat convection oven to 220°C / 425°F. Allow at least 45–60 minutes for the steel to fully saturate with heat.
2
Prepare lye solution. Wearing gloves and protective eyewear, add 6 g lye to 4 kg water in a large non-aluminum pot. Stir to dissolve. Begin bringing to a gentle boil about 10 minutes before the bagels are ready to boil.
3
Boil bagels straight from the refrigerator. Do not bring to room temperature first — the cold, firm dough holds its shape better in the boiling water, and the skin formed during cold proofing needs to go in intact. The boiling is the final expansion event, not a proof readiness check. Working in batches of 3–4, gently lower bagels directly from the refrigerator into the gently boiling lye solution. Boil 30 seconds on the first side, flip, and boil 30 seconds on the second side (1 minute total). Bagels may sink initially then float as they heat — this is normal. Remove with a slotted spoon and return to the cornmeal-dusted parchment-lined sheet pans, bottom side down.
4
Bake. Slide the sheet pan onto the baking steel. Bake at 220°C / 425°F convection until deep golden brown, 10–12 minutes. The methylcellulose holds the dough at maximum expansion giving a taut, smooth surface; the lye produces a shiny, slightly brittle crust.
5
Cool. Transfer to a wire rack and cool completely. Consume within 1 day or wrap and freeze for up to 2 months. To refresh: wrap in foil and bake at 175°C / 350°F until the crumb reaches 77°C / 170°F.

On grinding your own meat: Grinding from whole cuts lets you control fat content and ensures food safety — you can serve these medium-rare without worry. Ask your butcher to grind all three cuts together if you don't have a grinder, first through a large die then through a small die, alternating the meats. Keep everything cold throughout: grind into a bowl set over ice. Sharp blades are essential — dull blades ruin texture.

2

4

Hollandaise Sauce

Hollandaise Sauce

Yield ~300 g
Serve with blanched asparagus · eggs Benedict
Thomas Keller · MasterClass
Double boiler at home: Home stoves don't work well at a low setting — a double boiler spreads heat evenly, especially with pans without a heavy bottom. Keller's double-boiler process for the yolks takes about 2½ minutes.

Requires: Clarified butter held at 165°F / 74°C — see clarified butter recipe.
Temperature reference:
Yolk cooking stage — 63–70°C / 145–158°F: The safe window for cooking yolks. Below 63°C they won't thicken properly. Above 70°C they scramble — you'll see bits of curd. The fluffy ribbon stage happens in this window. The double boiler self-limits because steam caps at 100°C and the bowl buffers the heat.

Emulsification stage — 60–65°C / 140–149°F: Drop the temperature slightly after the yolks are cooked before adding butter. Warm enough to keep the emulsion active; cool enough that the yolks don't continue cooking as you build the sauce.

Clarified butter — 74°C / 165°F: Must be liquid and warm enough that adding it doesn't cool the sauce and break the emulsion. Critical for the double boiler method where there is no heat source under the bowl during emulsification.

Holding — 60°C / 140°F: Minimum safe holding temperature. Below this the emulsion becomes unstable and can break. Also the food safety floor for egg-based sauces.

Danger zone — above 70°C / 158°F: Yolk proteins over-coagulate. The sauce goes grainy, then breaks. This is why the double boiler method requires lifting the bowl on and off heat to stay in range.
⚠ If the sauce breaks: Return to heat to completely break it — the yolks will look like curd. Add a little fresh egg yolk, warm it, then slowly drizzle the broken sauce back in while whisking. Common causes: too much heat, moving too fast, or not enough water.

1

Emulsification science: An egg yolk can absorb an almost infinite amount of fat as long as moisture is maintained. Dijon mustard aids emulsification in addition to adding flavor. Add oil slowly — too fast overwhelms the yolk proteins and breaks the emulsion.

If it breaks: Add more Dijon mustard and whisk vigorously to re-emulsify. Prevention: slow drizzle, steady whisking, and moisture additions (water, vinegar, lemon) as the mayo thickens.

Whisking speed: Start slow. As the volume of the emulsion grows, you can whisk faster — the larger mass is more stable.

Stop when ready: Stop adding oil when you reach your desired viscosity and quantity. You should see peaks forming.

Rémoulade — capers are salty: Taste the mayonnaise first before seasoning the rémoulade — you may need little or no additional salt.

Beurre pommade: Butter brought to room temperature until it is soft and spreadable — almost the texture of mayonnaise. Do not melt it.

Slow, slow, slow: Keller's mantra for cooking eggs. The lowest possible heat on the stovetop, then a 250°F oven to finish — this produces the silken, consistently golden egg he is after.

Fines herbes: Equal parts flat-leaf parsley, chives, and tarragon, all finely minced. Fresh only — no dried substitutes.

Fillings: Sautéed mushrooms, melted leeks, or diced bacon all work well. Always warm the filling before adding to the omelet.

Make Omelet
1
Prepare eggs. Crack each egg into a small bowl first to remove any shell, then transfer to the blender beaker. Blend with the immersion blender until smooth and homogeneous. Pass through a chinois into a bowl.
2
Prepare pan. Brush the 8-inch nonstick pan generously with beurre pommade. Set over the lowest possible heat.
3

4

6

7
Finish. Brush lightly with beurre pommade for sheen. Garnish with additional fines herbes and Maldon salt. Serve immediately.

Peeling Tomatoes

Peeling Tomatoes

Technique blanch · shock · peel
Thomas Keller · MasterClass
Purpose: Tomatoes are blanched not to cook them but to loosen the skin for peeling. A slight curl along the score lines is the visual cue that they're ready to come out.

Timing: Start checking at 10 seconds. The skin should peel away easily from the scored cross. If it resists, return to the boiling water for another 5–10 seconds.

Concassé: Peeled, seeded, and diced tomato. Halve on the equator, squeeze out seeds, and dice to desired size. To dry it further for use in oil- or vinegar-dressed preparations, drain briefly in a strainer.

Cherry tomato salad: Blanch and peel cherry tomatoes, finish with extra-virgin olive oil and champagne or sherry vinegar, and season with Maldon salt just before serving.

Section 2
Blanch and Peel
1
Bring water to boil. Fill the stockpot with unsalted water and bring to a full boil. No salt — this is not a cooking blanch.
2
Score and core tomatoes. Using a paring knife, score a shallow cross pattern on the bottom of each tomato — do not cut deeply into the flesh. Use the tip of the knife to remove the woody green stem.
3
Blanch. Submerge the tomatoes in the boiling water. After 10 seconds, remove one and check — the skin should peel away easily from the scored cross. If it clings, return for another 5–10 seconds and check again. Look for a slight curl along the score lines as a visual cue.
4
Shock. Transfer to the ice bath to stop cooking. Once cool enough to handle, remove with a slotted spoon.
5
Peel. Use a paring knife to peel each tomato, working from the scored cross outward. The skin will slip off easily.
Section 3
Cut to Use
1
Whole or sliced. Use the peeled tomatoes whole or slice as needed.
2
Concassé. Slice the tomato in half on its equator. Gently squeeze the seeds from each half. Dice the seeded tomato to the desired size.
3
Dry concassé. To dry further for oil- or vinegar-dressed preparations, drain the diced tomato briefly in a strainer — this allows it to absorb dressing rather than dilute it.

Poached Eggs

Poached Eggs

Yield 2 eggs per batch
Max per batch 2–3 eggs
Thomas Keller · MasterClass
Vinegar is essential: The acidity helps set the egg white proteins quickly, giving the egg a clean shape. The water should taste slightly acidic but not sour — taste it before adding eggs.

Batch size: Poach only 2–3 eggs at a time to maintain the vortex and water temperature.

Make ahead: Poach eggs in advance, transfer to an ice bath, and reheat in simmering water for 30–45 seconds when ready to serve.

Serve on: Sliced brioche, blanched asparagus with hollandaise, or as part of eggs Benedict.

Section 2
Poach
1
Prepare water. Fill the saucepot with water and add 100 g distilled white vinegar. Bring to a gentle simmer. Taste — it should have a slight acidity.
2
Crack eggs into bowls. Crack each egg into a separate small bowl. This allows you to slip them in quickly and cleanly.
3
Create a vortex. Vigorously swirl the water around the edge of the pot to create a strong vortex.
4
Slip in eggs. One at a time, slip each egg into the center of the vortex. As the egg drops into the water it will set into a teardrop shape. Gently reinvigorate the vortex and repeat with the second egg. Poach no more than 2–3 eggs at a time.
5
Check doneness. After 2 minutes, gently lift an egg with the slotted spoon and press the yolk lightly — it should feel soft but set. If too soft, return to the water and check again in 30-second intervals.
6
Drain and trim. Once done, drain the egg on a paper towel. Use small kitchen scissors to trim off any stringy or ragged pieces of egg white.
7
Serve. Serve immediately on sliced brioche, or hold in an ice bath and reheat in simmering water for 30–45 seconds when ready to serve.

Potato Purée

Potato Purée
Pommes Purée

Yield ~4 portions
Thomas Keller · MasterClass
Potato choice: La Ratte fingerling potatoes are ideal — their dry, fine-textured flesh produces the smoothest purée and absorbs the most fat. Yukon Gold is the best widely available substitute. Avoid russets — their coarse starch granules produce a gummy texture when puréed.

Cook whole: Potatoes are cooked whole and unpeeled to prevent water absorption, which allows more butter and cream to be incorporated.

Work hot: Pass the potatoes through the tamis while they are still hot and moist — cold potato will not pass smoothly and will not take in fat properly.

Emulsion: If the purée looks oily and the fat is separating, the emulsion is breaking. Add a splash of hot water and adjust heat — the same principle as hollandaise or mayonnaise.

Clarified butter note: Clarified butter adds more intense butter flavor than whole butter. Use it in addition to, not instead of, the whole butter cubes.

Variation: Stir in a spoonful of crème fraîche at the end for added acidity and richness.

190 gheavy cream, hot
50 gclarified butter (optional — see note)

kosher salt, to taste
unsalted butter, for finishing (a small pat)
Maldon salt, for finishing

1stiff rubber spatula
1slotted spoon
1paring knife (for doneness test)
1serving bowl
parchment (under tamis to catch pulp)
kitchen scale
Section 2
Cook Potatoes
1
Start in cold water. Place the whole, unpeeled 750 g potatoes in a 3-qt saucepot. Cover by 2 inches with cold water. Slowly bring to a gentle simmer — starting in cold water ensures even cooking throughout.
2
Cook until extremely tender. Simmer until a paring knife slides into the center of a potato with absolutely no resistance. Turn off the heat.
Section 3
Pass and Build Purée
1
Pass through tamis immediately. Working one potato at a time while still hot, remove from the water, place on the tamis set over parchment, split in half, and press the flesh through the screen using a stiff bowl scraper. Leave the skins behind. Transfer the passed pulp to the 4-qt saucepan.
2
Warm the pan. Set the saucepan over medium-low heat. Use a stiff rubber spatula throughout — it gives the control needed to incorporate fat properly.
3
Incorporate cream and butter in stages. Add ⅓ of the hot cream and beat it into the potatoes. Then beat in a few cubes of cold butter until fully emulsified. Drizzle in a little clarified butter. Continue alternating cream, whole butter, and clarified butter, beating well after each addition, until you reach your desired texture. The finished purée should be milky and creamy — not oily.
4
If the emulsion breaks. If the purée looks greasy and separated, add a splash of hot water and beat to restore the emulsion. Regulate the heat — too high will break it faster.
5
Season and serve. Season with kosher salt to taste. Transfer to a serving bowl and top with a small pat of butter and a pinch of Maldon salt.

Smoked Brisket

Smoked Brisket

Yield serves 10–15
Total time ~12 hours + up to 2 hours rest
Cut packer brisket (point + flat), ~12 lbs
Aaron Franklin · MasterClass
Smoke absorption: Brisket stops meaningfully absorbing smoke at around 140–170°F internal temperature — the point at which myoglobin denatures and can no longer react with smoke gases. At 255°F, a 12 lb packer brisket reaches this range within the first 3–4 hours, during Stage 1. This is why Franklin says after wrapping you no longer need clean smoke and can burn junkier wood — the interior is already as smoky as it's going to get. Focus all fire management on hours 0–3. Clean, thin, blue-white smoke during this window is the single most important factor in the brisket's flavor. Thick white or black smoke deposits bitter creosote. After the wrap, concentrate on temperature only.
About the cut: A full packer brisket has two muscles — the flat (leaner, rectangular) and the point (fattier, bulbous) — separated by a layer of seam fat. Always buy a packer cut with both. Look for good marbling; prime grades have the most. The brisket should feel firm but not stiff — too rigid often means little marbling. Avoid thick, hard fat caps (indicates industrial feeding), previously frozen cuts (mushy, saggy feel; lots of blood in Cryovac packaging), and briskets where the flat and point are very different in size (uneven cooking).

Trim cold, cook at room temp: Trim the brisket cold — fat becomes sticky and hard to work with as it warms. After trimming, let the brisket rest at room temperature while you build the fire.

Wrap options: Butcher paper is preferred — more breathable than foil, traps less steam, keeps the brisket moist without making the bark soggy. Foil works but softens the bark. Unwrapped gives the crunchiest bark but requires careful monitoring to prevent drying out.

Section 2
Trim (Do Cold)
1
Orient the brisket. The goal of every trim is a perfect balance of meat, fat, bark, and smoke in each slice. Aim for smooth, aerodynamic curves — no boxy lines, no 90-degree angles. Leave a little extra fat if it means preserving structural integrity. It's always better to leave too much than have the brisket fall apart in the smoker.
2
Trim the sides. One long edge will be rougher in texture with slight discoloration — the side split from the carcass during processing. It won't take on smoke or flavor. Remove about ½ inch with two or three long smooth strokes of the boning knife. Do the same on the other side to expose the flat and even the shape. If the flat end is very thin, trim off a couple inches to reach meat thick enough to survive the cook without drying out.
3
Trim the fat cap (fat side up). Look for dimples on the fat cap near the center — that's where the fat cap ends. Cut in at a curved angle and move the blade along the length of the flat, peeling back the top layer with your other hand. You're exposing the soft white fat beneath the waxy exterior. Shave down until you have a uniform layer of pillowy fat approximately ¼ inch thick. Trim some hardened fat from the side of the point. Leave the thick pocket of seam fat directly beneath the point — removing it before the cook will cause the point to collapse. You'll trim it at slicing time.
4
Trim the underside (fat cap down). Flip the brisket. The big chunk of exposed seam fat where the flat and point connect is now visible. Make a few entry cuts, then peel and slice it off the same way you did the fat cap. Remove any stray bits of fatty membrane.
5
Remove anything weird. If present, cut off the deckle — a thick layer of hard fat connecting the flat to the rib cage that contributes nothing. Remove any silverskin (shiny connective tissue) and any strange unidentifiable muscle bits left from butchering.
Section 3
Slather and Rub
1
Season lean side first. The fat side is the presentation side — do it last. Starting fat side down, slather the lean side with mustard, hot sauce, or water — just wet enough for the rub to adhere. Shake the rub from side to side in an even layer. Avoid filling deep pockets or gaps with salt and pepper. Gently pat the rub in.
2
Season the sides. Cup your free hand along one long edge of the brisket. Pour rub into your hand and move along the length, pressing it evenly into the side. Repeat on the other side.
3
Season fat cap last. Flip fat side up. Slather, then sprinkle rub on top, patting it in at the end.
4
Rest at room temperature for 30–40 minutes while you build the fire and bring the smoker to temperature.
Section 4
Stage 1 — Cook Undisturbed (Hours 0–3)
1
Bring smoker to 255°F with clean, light smoke — a faint bluish hue. These first hours establish the brisket's flavor base. Fire management and smoke quality are the priority.
2
Place brisket point-toward-fire. The point is fattier and more forgiving — it can handle more heat. Shut the lid and cook undisturbed for 3 hours at a constant 255°F. Do not open.
Section 5
Stage 2 — First Check and Spritz (Hours 3–6)
1
Check at 3 hours. The brisket should have a mahogany hue and consistent bark. Warning signs: burning, splotchy bark, dry/crisp patches, or fat already rendering → cut back on heat. Discoloration without dryness or rendered fat → likely dirty smoke, monitor wood quality closely.
2
Spritz vulnerable edges to cool them and add moisture.
3
Raise temperature to 260–265°F (unless the fire has already been running too hot). Cook for another 3 hours, checking and spritzing the dry edges once per hour.
Section 6
Stage 3 — Push Through the Stall (~Hour 6)
1
Understand the stall. Around 165°F internal, the brisket hits evaporative cooling — muscles tighten and force moisture to the surface, cooling the meat. The brisket is technically well done at 165°F but would be impossibly tough. Collagen begins converting to gelatin only above 180°F. You must push through.
2
Ramp up temperature to 280–285°F just before the stall hits. Don't worry about burning — the moisture rising to the surface counteracts the higher heat. Cook at this temperature for approximately 1 hour.
3
Check for stiffness. Lift the brisket and flex it — if it bends at the edges, you're through the stall and ready to assess for wrapping.
Section 7
Stage 4 — The Wrap (~Hour 7)
1
Judge readiness by the flat. The flat is your barometer — the point has more margin for error. Lift the edge of the flat from underneath with your fingers; when it feels firm but a little floppy, it's ready to wrap. Bark cracking in places also means fat is rendering — wrap time.
2
Set up wrap. Lay two sheets of wide butcher paper (or foil) on a clean workstation — each sheet four times the width of the brisket. Overlap them by about half their width, long edge perpendicular to you.
3
Position brisket. Lay the brisket lengthwise across the paper, presentation side up, about 1 foot from the bottom edge. Spritz anywhere that needs moisture. Spritz the paper surface lightly.
4
Wrap tightly — 6 steps: (1) Fold the bottom edge of the paper over the top of the brisket and pull as tight as possible — every fold should conform to the brisket's shape. (2) Fold one side tightly over the flat at an obtuse angle away from you, smoothing the paper. (3) Tuck part of the paper on the opposite side under the point to secure it, then fold in over the top at an obtuse angle and smooth. (4) Hold the wrap tight on all sides, roll the brisket over, and pull tightly to secure — fold in the sides again. (5) Fold the top end of the paper over to double its thickness. (6) Roll the brisket one final time — the presentation side should now face up with a double layer of wrap beneath it and paper tight on all sides.
Section 8
Stage 5 — Finish Wrapped (Hours 8–12)
1
Return to smoker, point toward fire. At this stage the brisket won't absorb any more smoke flavor. Concentrate on temperature only — clean fire no longer matters. Use whatever wood you have, including lower-quality pieces you've been holding back.
2
Cook at 275–285°F for ~3 hours, then gradually allow the temperature to taper off for the final hour. Residual heat will continue cooking the brisket after it comes off — factor this into your timing.
Section 9
Stage 6 — Pull and Rest
1
Check every 15 minutes. Using a towel to protect your hands, pick up the brisket and run your fingers along its length. As collagen breaks down the brisket becomes increasingly pliable. When it feels loose, flexible, and slightly jiggly throughout, it's done. Pull it too soon rather than leave it on too long.
2
Rest in wrapping until the internal temperature drops to 140–150°F. Carryover cooking will continue during this time. Factor in at least 30 minutes and up to 1–2 hours depending on ambient temperature and how hot the smoker was running. Do not slice until ready to serve — once cut, the meat begins oxidizing immediately.
Section 10
Slice and Serve
1
Knife and technique. Use a 12-inch serrated bread knife. Push and pull through the meat in smooth, even strokes with minimal pressure. Unlike other knife work, use your guide hand to shape and protect the meat rather than curling your knuckles — be aware of where your fingers are at all times. Note that push strokes help keep a drier, more fragile slice intact.
2
Slice the flat. Starting from the corner opposite the point, slice the flat against the grain at an angle. Each slice should be about the width of a No. 2 pencil. As you work along the length of the flat, gradually taper the slice thickness so that by the end the slices are perfectly straight across.
3
Prepare the point. Cut off and discard the thick pocket of seam fat on one side of the point that was left in place during the cook. Slice the point in half lengthwise — this is the "money shot." Both the marbling and the seam fat running through the center should have rendered perfectly. Evaluate: the front section (closest to the fire) should not be dry or crunchy; the fat cap should be less than ¼ inch thick. Note any issues for next time.
4
Slice the point. Point slices should be slightly thicker than the flat — about ⅜ inch. Slice straight across the muscle, then gradually taper on the side closest to you so slices are a little thicker on one end. Repeat with both halves, adjusting for any pockets that are too fatty or too dry. Under one half you'll find another thick seam fat pocket — trim it off.
5
Handle the end pieces. The final flat piece beneath the seam fat and the ridge of the point above it won't make great individual slices. Add the flat piece to beans or serve as chopped beef. Cut the ridge of the point — burnt ends — into bite-size snacks.

Smoked Pork Butt

Smoked Pork Butt

Yield serves 8–12
Total time ~10 hours + 1 hour rest
Cut bone-in pork butt, 8–10 lbs
Aaron Franklin · MasterClass
Smoke absorption: Pork butt stops meaningfully absorbing smoke at around 140–170°F internal temperature — the point at which myoglobin denatures and can no longer react with smoke gases to form the smoke ring. At 270°F, this typically happens within the first 3–4 hours of the cook, well within Stage 1 and early Stage 2. After that point, smoke continues to build and darken the bark but is no longer penetrating deeper into the meat. This is why wrapping at Stage 3 costs you nothing in smoke flavor — the interior is already as smoky as it's going to get. Focus your best fire management on hours 0–4: that is your window. Clean, thin, blue-white smoke during this period is what you're after — thick white or black smoke deposits bitter creosote on the surface. After the wrap, smoke quality becomes much less critical.
About the cut: Pork butt (Boston butt) is the upper front shoulder — heavily marbled with fat and connective tissue. The fat makes it extremely forgiving and less prone to drying out than brisket, even at higher temperatures. Always buy bone-in — boneless butts have a less uniform shape that cooks unevenly. When done, the bone slides out clean.

Fat trimming: Unlike brisket, you don't need to trim excess surface fat before the cook — since you'll be pulling rather than slicing, any unrendered pockets of fat can be removed by hand after. Skin, however, must come off before cooking — it blocks smoke and rub penetration and turns inedibly tough.

Seasoning: Pork butt is almost impossible to over-season. However salty or peppery the bark is, it gets pulled and mixed with the less seasoned interior.

Section 2
Prep
1
Remove skin. If the pork butt has skin on, remove it entirely — it will block smoke and rub penetration and turn too tough to eat. Ask your butcher to do this if preferred.
2
Make rub. Mix equal parts kosher salt and 16-mesh café grind black pepper with a small amount of paprika for color. You'll need about ½ cup total for an 8–10 lb butt.
3
Slather and rub — non-presentation side first. The fattier side is the presentation side — do it last. Starting with the lean side, slather with mustard or hot sauce, then sprinkle the rub in an even layer. Slather and rub the sides, cupping one hand along the edge to catch and press the rub evenly onto the meat. Flip fat-side up. Slather and rub the fat cap last.
4
Rest. Allow the seasoned pork butt to rest at room temperature for 30–40 minutes to let the rub penetrate and begin drawing out internal moisture.
Section 3
Stage 1 — Cook Undisturbed (Hours 0–3)
1
Bring smoker to temperature. Get the smoker to a consistent 270°F and producing clean smoke before the meat goes on.
2
Place fat cap up. Set the pork butt in the cooking chamber fat cap facing up. The high fat content means you don't need to start lower as you would with brisket.
3
Cook undisturbed for 3 hours at 270°F, tending the fire and maintaining a constant temperature and the cleanest possible smoke throughout.
Section 4
Stage 2 — Spritz Hourly (Hours 3–8)
1
First spritz at 3 hours. Open the smoker and give the pork butt a thorough spritzing of water, beer, or apple cider vinegar to cool the exterior and add moisture.
2
Continue at 270°F for ~5 more hours, checking and spritzing once per hour. As fat renders the butt will gradually shrink, and the bark on top of the fat cap will eventually split — this is the signal to wrap.
Section 5
Stage 3 — The Wrap (~Hour 8)
1
Set up foil. Lay two sheets of wide aluminum foil, each four times as long as the widest side of the butt, on a clean surface. Overlap the sheets by about half their width, shiny side up.
2
Position pork butt. Lay the pork butt on the foil fat side up, about 8 inches from the bottom edge, with the long side running parallel to the bottom edge of the foil. Give the pork one last spritz, then lightly spritz the foil surface.
3
Wrap tightly — 5 steps: (1) Fold the bottom of the foil tightly over the top of the pork. (2) Fold both sides at an obtuse angle — tight but with sides that can fold again. (3) Roll the pork over, fold both sides inward again. (4) Roll once more, tuck in any excess foil. (5) Feel the entire wrap — the foil should conform tightly to the meat with no air pockets. When placed back in the smoker, the duller side of the foil should face out to absorb rather than reflect heat.
Section 6
Stage 4 — Finish Wrapped (Hours 8–10)
1
Return to smoker. Place the wrapped pork butt back in the smoker. Cook for 1 hour at 270°F, then raise the temperature to 295°F and cook for 1 final hour.
Section 7
Stage 5 — Pull and Rest
1
Check doneness. After 10 hours, the pork butt should register just over 200°F internal temperature. Poke through the wrap with a thermometer — make as few holes as possible. Alternatively judge by feel: pick it up and move it around — it should feel squishy and soft throughout. Any spots that don't feel quite right will continue to cook during the rest.
2
Rest. Allow the wrapped pork butt to rest at room temperature for 1 hour before serving.
Section 8
Pull and Serve
1
Unwrap carefully. Open the foil carefully so the trapped juices don't spill. Pour all the accumulated juices over the pork.
2
Pull. Shred the pork using your fingers or tongs. The bone should slide out cleanly — discard it or save for stock. Remove any large unrendered pockets of fat by hand, or chop them up and mix into the meat.
3
Serve.

Aaron Franklin · MasterClass
Smoke absorption: Spare ribs stop meaningfully absorbing smoke at around 140–170°F internal temperature — the point at which myoglobin denatures and can no longer react with smoke gases. At 265–270°F, a rack of spare ribs hits this range within the first 2–3 hours of the cook, well within Stage 1. After that, smoke continues to build and darken the bark but is not penetrating deeper into the meat. This is why the wrap at Stage 3 costs you nothing in smoke flavor. Focus your best fire management on hours 0–3: that is your window. Clean, thin, blue-white smoke during this period is essential — thick white or black smoke deposits bitter creosote. After the wrap, smoke quality becomes much less critical.
About spare ribs: Spare ribs come from the lower rib cage near the belly and sternum — fattier and more flavorful than baby backs. Difficulty falls between pork butt and brisket: consistent temperature throughout but less margin for error due to the thinner cut. This is the only Franklin cook that uses sauce, applied just before wrapping so it emulsifies with the rendering fat.

Shopping: Look for white fat and red-pink meat. Avoid dry or gray racks, exposed bones on the surface (known as "shiners" — a sign of bad butchering), "enhanced" pork injected with salt water (less flavor control, heavier, more expensive), and excessive blood in the packaging (previously frozen).

3-3 game plan: Franklin uses a "3-3" — 3 hours unwrapped, 3 hours wrapped — rather than the common "3-2-1" method. The ribs stay wrapped for the entire second half.

Section 2
Make Aaron's Rib Sauce (Make Ahead)
1
Cook onion and garlic. Heat a saucepan over medium heat and add 2 tbsp fat. Once shimmering, add the onion and cook until soft and translucent, 6–8 minutes. Add the garlic and continue cooking until the onion starts to brown and garlic turns crisp, about 3 minutes more.
2
Add sugar. Add the 1 cup brown sugar and cook, stirring frequently, until the sugar melts and starts to form a glaze, about 2–3 minutes.
3
Add remaining ingredients. Stir in 1 cup apple cider vinegar, 2 cups ketchup, 1 tsp smoked paprika, 1 tsp mustard powder, 1 tsp salt, and 1 tsp pepper. Bring to a simmer and cook for 3–5 minutes until slightly thickened. Add 4 dashes Worcestershire and simmer for 1 more minute.
4
Blend. Transfer to a blender. Pulse once or twice before going to full speed — hot liquids splatter. Blend on high for about 1 minute until smooth and orange-hued. Transfer to a heat-resistant container and cool to room temperature. Refrigerate for up to 1 month.
Section 3
Trim
1
Orient the rack. Lay the rack parallel to the bottom edge of your cutting board. The top edge should be a roughly straight line; the bottom edge curves where the ribs connected to the sternum. The presentation side (outside) looks cleaner and meatier; the inside is where you'll find the skirt, silverskin, and fat pockets.
2
Remove sternum and cartilage. Cut the sternum (breastbone) off at an angle. Clean the bottom edge with a thin slice along the length of the rack, removing stray bits of flesh and cartilage to leave a smooth, slightly curved edge. Round off the far end of the rack opposite the sternum for a clean, aerodynamic shape.
3
Silverskin. The thin white membrane on the inside of the rack. Franklin leaves it on — it doesn't block flavor penetration. Removing it is optional and a matter of preference.
4
Remove the skirt. The diagonal flap of meat on the inside. If the butcher hasn't already removed it, trim it off. It's good meat — save it for sausage or beans.
5
Check for anything weird. Feel all around the rack for stray chips of bone or cartilage left from butchering — remove them with your fingers. Trim any random connective tissue. Trim excessive fat only if you're confident it improves the rack.
Section 4
Slather and Rub
1
Mix rub. Combine 2 parts black pepper : 1 part kosher salt plus a small amount of paprika. You'll need about ¼ cup per rack. The pepper-forward ratio suits ribs as a thinner cut.
2
Season inside first. Starting with the non-presentation (inside) surface, slather with mustard or hot sauce — texture should be tacky, not wet, on the meatier parts; slightly heavier on the fattier portions. Sprinkle the rub in an even layer moving parallel to the rib bones. Use one hand for slathering and turning, the other for applying rub.
3
Season presentation side. Flip to the fat side (presentation side) and repeat — slightly heavier slather and rub on fattier sections, which helps smoke adhere and gives more uniform flavor.
4
Rest. Allow the rack to rest at room temperature while you build the fire and bring the smoker to temperature.
Section 5
Stage 1 — Cook Unwrapped (Hours 0–3)
1
Bring smoker to temperature. Reach a consistent 265–270°F with clean smoke before the ribs go on.
2
Place ribs. Set the rack in the cooking chamber with the thickest part closest to the fire.
3
Spritz edges hourly. At the end of hours 1 and 2, spritz the edges with water, beer, or apple cider vinegar to cool them and maintain moisture.
4
Watch the fat. If at any point during the first 3 hours the fat looks like it's starting to render, reduce your cooking temperature. The goal is to have the fat render only after wrapping, where it will emulsify with the sauce.
Section 6
Stage 2 — Apply Sauce (~Hour 2½)
1
Dilute the sauce. After hour 2 of cooking, combine equal parts warm rib sauce and apple cider vinegar in a squeeze bottle and shake well. Diluting thins the sauce and reduces sugar content, making it less likely to burn in the smoker.
2
Wet the surface. At 2½ hours into the cook, thoroughly spritz the rack until the surface is wet to the touch.
3
Apply sauce — presentation side. Squeeze the diluted sauce in an even layer parallel to the bones. Work it around the sides and surface by hand until the entire presentation side is coated. Give it one more light spritz, close the lid, and let the sauce set for about 10 minutes.
4
Flip and repeat. Flip the rack and repeat the saucing process on the other side. Let set for 10 more minutes.
Section 7
Stage 3 — The Wrap (~Hour 3)
1
Remove ribs from smoker. Use your hands, not tongs — jagged metal can cut into the rack. Spritz your hands first to cool the skin, or use a towel. Give the rack a thorough once-over: pull out any small bone chips that have emerged as the meat shrank (they'll puncture the foil). Note if the meat feels too dry or sauce is burning — if so, consider lowering heat for the final stages.
2
Set up foil. Cut a sheet of wide aluminum foil twice the length of the rack. Lay on your workstation, longer edge parallel to you, shiny side up. The dull side will face out in the smoker to absorb rather than reflect heat.
3
Sauce the foil. Spritz the foil and spread a little more barbecue sauce on top. Lay the ribs presentation side down on top of the sauce, about 3 inches from the bottom of the foil. Give the non-presentation side one last spritz.
4
Wrap tightly — 5 steps: (1) Fold one end of the foil over the ribs, then the other — both ends overlap in the center. (2) Tuck the foil tightly around the bottom edge of the rack until it conforms to the shape. (3) Locate the diagonal edge where the sternum was; fold the foil over the top and tuck tight. Repeat with the opposite corner. (4) Tuck the foil tightly around the top of the rack. (5) Fold the top edge over and tightly fold in both sides at an obtuse angle — wrapped tight but sides can fold once more if needed.
Section 8
Stage 4 — Cook Wrapped (Hours 3–6)
1
Return to smoker. Place the wrapped rack back in the smoker with the seam of the foil facing up. Cook at 265–270°F for 3 hours.
Section 9
Stage 5 — Rest, Slice, and Serve
1
Check doneness. After 6 hours, remove the rack and set on your workstation seam-side up. Feel around the sternum area for the third rib bone — if the meat around it feels soft and pliable, the ribs are done.
2
Rest. Allow the wrapped ribs to rest for 30–40 minutes.
3
Unwrap and flip. Carefully unwrap without spilling the juices. With the bottom and sternum edge facing away from you, grab the far end of the foil and lift it up to flip the ribs toward you — they should land presentation side up on your cutting board with the juices pouring over the top.
4
Slice and serve. Using a chef's knife, slice the rack between each bone. Serve.

Buttermilk Bread

Buttermilk Bread

Yield 2 loaves · ~680 g each
Mixing 8–10 minutes
Total time ~3½–4 hours
DDT 80°F / 27°C
Peter Reinhart · The Bread Baker's Apprentice · Variation 2
Buttermilk: Substitutes directly for water in Reinhart's Variation 1 base formula. The acidity tenderizes the crumb and adds a subtle tang. Add more buttermilk or flour as needed while mixing — the dough should be soft, supple, and tacky but not sticky.

Enriched dough baking temperatures: Never bake at the high temperatures used for lean hearth breads — the enrichments cause rapid caramelization. Loaves: 325°F convection (350°F conventional). Rolls and buns: 375°F convection (400°F conventional).

Doneness: Rolls and buns → internal temp just above 180°F. Loaves → internal temp 185–190°F, golden brown on top and sides, hollow when thumped on the bottom.

Fat substitutions: Shortening gives the softest texture; butter gives the best flavor. Margarine or vegetable oil also work — all tenderize the bread.

Other shapes from this dough: 18 dinner rolls (56 g / 2 oz each) · 12 burger or hot dog buns (85 g / 3 oz each).

3
Cool. Remove immediately from the pans and cool on a wire rack for at least 1 hour before slicing.

Cinnamon Raisin Bread

Cinnamon Raisin Bread

Yield 1 large loaf or 2 small loaves
Active time 9 min
Total time ~4 hours 28 min
DDT 24–26°C / 75–78°F
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 6
Pan sizes: Large loaf: 33 × 10 × 10 cm / 13 × 4 × 4 inches. Small loaves: two 20 × 11.5 × 7.5 cm / 7¾ × 4¼ × 3 inch pans.

Osmotolerant yeast required in the dough: The enrichments (sugar, butter, egg) inhibit standard yeast. Use SAF Gold or equivalent.

Cold milk and cold egg: Both are added cold — this helps control dough temperature and keeps the butter from melting prematurely during mixing.

Cinnamon smear application: After preshaped into a rectangle, spread the smear evenly across the surface, then scatter raisins and cinnamon over the smear before rolling up. The smear is what keeps the swirl layers distinct and moist.

Egg wash twice: Before proofing (after shaping) and again just before scoring and baking.

Glazing: Once baked and fully cooled, apply buttermilk icing in thin parallel lines across the top, then dust with ground cinnamon.

Storage: Consume within 1 day, or freeze for up to 2 months.

1

French Lean Bread

French Lean Bread

Yield ~1 kg
Active time 27 min (machine)
Total time ~6 hours
DDT 24–26°C / 75–78°F

Poolish: Mix 12 hours before baking and ferment in an airtight container at 21–24°C / 70–75°F for 12–16 hours. It should be aerated and frothy with a slightly tangy smell.

Salt withholding (machine mixing): Disperse salt in 15 g of the Group ② water and set aside — this salt solution is added after autolyse as the final mix step. It helps distribute salt more evenly throughout the dough.

Spiral mixer minimum: This recipe yields ~1 kg. Spiral mixers require at least 8 kg — multiply by 8 or more for spiral mixing. For a stand mixer (8 qt bowl), multiply by 2 but do not exceed 2.5×.

Crispy crust requires steam. A home oven without steam injection can approximate this using a cast-iron combination cooker or a baking stone with a large lid. See baking table below.

Storage: Consume within 1 day, or freeze for up to 2 months.

Hamburger Buns

Yield 12 buns · 100 g each · ~33 g overage
Active time ~9 minutes mixing
Total time ~3 hours 47 minutes
DDT 24–26°C / 75–78°F
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 6
Desired dough temperature (DDT): 24–26°C / 75–78°F. Use cold milk and adjust ingredient temperatures to hit this target — it controls fermentation rate and timing.

Doneness: Baking times vary. The most reliable method is internal temperature: buns are done when the core reads 90–93°C / 195–200°F.

Storage: Consume within 1–2 days, or freeze for up to 2 months.

Variations: Same dough makes hot dog buns (divide into 100 g pieces, shape as 15 cm oblong bâtards, proof 45–60 min at 27°C, bake at 195°C / 385°F for 20–25 min) and 100% whole wheat buns (soak 85 g toasted bran + 15 g toasted germ in 100 g water for 10 min, add after full gluten development).

Section 2
Make Sponge (30 min–1 hour ahead)
1
Mix sponge. Combine 340 g bread flour, 296 g whole milk, and 6.3 g instant yeast in the mixer bowl. Mix on low speed until a cohesive mass forms.
2
Ferment. Cover the bowl tightly with plastic wrap. Ferment at 27°C / 80°F for 30 minutes, or at 21°C / 70°F for 1 hour.
Section 3
Mix Dough
1
First mix — medium gluten development. Add the 636 g sponge, 340 g bread flour, 151 g cold milk, 50 g honey, 12.6 g salt, and 2.5 g malt syrup to the mixer bowl. Mix on medium speed until the dough reaches medium gluten development — it should be smooth and slightly elastic but not yet window-paning.
2
Add butter — full gluten development. Add 75 g softened butter and continue mixing on medium speed until full gluten development — the dough should be smooth, elastic, and pass the windowpane test.
3
Transfer. Move the dough to a lightly oiled tub or bowl. Cover well with a lid or plastic wrap.
Section 4
Bulk Ferment (1 hour)
1
Ferment 30 minutes, then perform one four-edge fold: working with wet hands, stretch and fold each of the four edges of the dough over to the center, then flip seam-side down.
2
Rest 30 more minutes. Cover and complete the bulk ferment. Total bulk ferment time: 1 hour.
Section 5
Divide and Shape
1
Divide. Turn the dough onto an unfloured surface. Divide into 12 pieces of 100 g each, with ~33 g remaining.
2
Shape into round rolls. For each piece, flatten slightly, then fold the edges in toward the center to create surface tension. Flip seam-side down and roll under a cupped hand in tight circles on an unfloured surface until a smooth, tight ball forms.
3
Pan and egg wash. Place the shaped buns in a hamburger bun pan or on a parchment-lined sheet pan with ample space between them. Apply egg wash now, before proofing.
Section 6
Final Proof
1
Proof. At 27°C / 80°F (65% RH): 1–1¼ hours. At 21°C / 70°F: 1½–2 hours. The buns are ready when they have visibly increased in size, feel light and pillowy, and spring back slowly when gently pressed.
2
Second egg wash and seeds. Just before baking, apply a second coat of egg wash and sprinkle with poppy, sesame, or nigella seeds if using.
Section 7
Bake
1
Convection oven: Preheat to 175°C / 350°F. Bake for 12–15 minutes until deep golden brown.
2
Regular (non-convection) oven: Preheat to 190°C / 375°F. Bake for 25–30 minutes until deep golden brown.
3
Check internal temperature. The buns are done when the center reads 90–93°C / 195–200°F. Transfer to a wire rack and cool completely before slicing.

Hoagie Buns

Hoagie Buns

Yield 8 buns · 125 g each
Active time 18 min
Total time ~4 hours 30 min
DDT 24–26°C / 75–78°F
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 6
Pâte fermentée: Make 12 hours ahead and refrigerate. Remove 2 hours before mixing to come to room temperature.

Regular instant yeast (not osmotolerant) is used in both the pâte fermentée and the dough — unlike the kaiser rolls. The sugar level is slightly lower here, keeping the dough within range for standard yeast.

No bulk ferment folds: Unlike the kaiser, hoagie dough has no folds during bulk ferment — keep covered throughout and let it ferment undisturbed for 1½ hours.

Egg wash sequence: Apply egg wash before shaping into the final bâtard — not before proofing. Apply egg wash again just before scoring. Score a single cut down the center.

Whole wheat variation: Replace 20% of the bread flour with whole wheat flour.

Daily harvesting: If making hoagies regularly, harvest a piece of dough each day and use it as the pâte fermentée for the next batch.

Storage: Consume within 1–2 days, or freeze for up to 2 months.

Kaiser Rolls

Kaiser Rolls

Yield 8 rolls · 125 g each
Active time 18 min
Total time ~4 hours 23 min
DDT 24–26°C / 75–78°F
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 6
Pâte fermentée: Make 12 hours ahead and refrigerate. Remove from refrigerator 2 hours before mixing to come to room temperature. The pâte fermentée adds depth and complexity of flavor that fast-acting enriched doughs lack.

Osmotolerant yeast required in the dough (not the pâte fermentée): The enrichments (sugar, fat) inhibit standard yeast. Use SAF Gold or equivalent. Regular instant yeast is used for the pâte fermentée only.

Egg wash before proofing: Apply egg wash, then press poppy seeds into the surface, then proof. This anchors the seeds during proofing so they adhere cleanly after baking.

Daily harvesting: If making kaiser rolls regularly, harvest a piece of dough each day and use it as the pâte fermentée for the next batch.

Storage: Consume within 1–2 days, or freeze for up to 2 months.

Lemon-Glazed Pound Cake

Lemon-Glazed
Pound Cake

Yield 16 servings
Prep 20 minutes
Bake 35–60 minutes (pan dependent)
King Arthur Baking
Butter temperature: Butter must be at least 65°F / 18°C — genuinely soft, not just out of the fridge. Cold butter won't cream properly and will produce a dense cake.

Pan options and bake times: Two 8½ × 4½-inch loaf pans → 35–40 minutes. One tube or bundt pan → 55–60 minutes.

Glaze sugar: King Arthur Baker's Special Sugar (superfine / castor sugar) dissolves faster and produces a cleaner glaze. Regular granulated works fine — just heat until fully dissolved.

Lemon flavoring: Use ½ tsp lemon oil or ¼ tsp Fiori di Sicilia — both are more stable and concentrated than zest alone.

Section 2
Make Cake
1
Preheat oven to 350°F. Grease the pan(s).
2
Cream butter and cream cheese. Beat together 198 g butter and 85 g cream cheese until soft and fluffy.
3
Add dry ingredients and beat. Add ½ tsp salt, 298 g sugar, 206 g flour, and 1 tsp baking powder. Beat for 5 minutes — the batter will be stiff.
4
Add flavorings and eggs. Add 2 tsp vanilla, ½ tsp lemon oil, and 1 egg. Beat well. Continue adding the remaining 4 eggs one at a time, beating well and scraping the sides and bottom of the bowl after each addition. The finished batter will be very fluffy.
5
Pan and bake. Spoon the batter into the prepared pan(s). Bake until a toothpick inserted in the center comes out clean: 35–40 minutes for loaf pans · 55–60 minutes for tube or bundt pan.
Section 3
Make Glaze and Finish
1
Make glaze. Just before the cake comes out of the oven, combine 57 g lemon juice and 99 g sugar in a small saucepan over low heat (or microwave). Heat until the sugar is fully dissolved — do not boil.
2
Cool and unmold. Remove the cake from the oven and cool in the pan for 10 minutes. Turn out onto a wire rack or serving platter.
3
Apply glaze. Poke the top of the cake all over with a toothpick or cake tester. Gradually drizzle the glaze over the warm cake, pausing occasionally to let it sink in.
4
Cool completely. Allow the cake to cool for several hours before slicing — the glaze needs time to set and the crumb to firm up.

Loaded Nachos

Loaded Nachos

Yield serves 2–4
Joshua Weissman · But Cheaper
Beef: Use the meat filling from the Crispy Tacos (Tacos Dorados) recipe — often kept frozen and thawed as needed. No additional beef prep required here.

Cheese sauce: Use a cheese whose main ingredient is dairy fat, not starch fillers. American cheese is the emulsifying agent — it prevents the cheddar from breaking and going grainy. Do not skip it.

Drain and season. Remove with a spider and place on a wire rack over a baking sheet. Season with salt immediately while hot. Repeat with remaining tortillas.
Section 3

1
Make roux. In a medium saucepan over medium heat, melt 21 g butter. Add 15 g flour, whisk together, and cook while stirring for 30–40 seconds.
2
Add milk and spices. Whisk in 237 g milk. Once it begins to thicken, add paprika, cumin, and garlic powder.
3
Melt cheese. Whisk in 72 g American cheese and 72 g cheddar, continuing to whisk until smooth. If too thick, add a splash of milk. Season with salt and pepper.
Section 4

2

3

2
Second layer. Add the remaining chips on top and repeat — more cheese sauce, a light dollop of sour cream, the guacamole, and a final garnish of cilantro leaves. Serve immediately.

Modernist French Lean Bread

Modernist French
Lean Bread

Yield ~1 kg
Active time 34 min
Total time ~5¾ hours
DDT 24–26°C / 75–78°F
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 6
Vacuum chamber required. This recipe uses a vacuum chamber for mixing — the autolyse and gluten development happen under vacuum rather than through mechanical kneading. A vacuum chamber is not optional; without it the dough will be under-developed. Use the standard French Lean Bread recipe instead if a vacuum chamber is unavailable.
Water temperature: The vacuum chamber cools the dough (unlike a mixer, which adds heat through friction). Use water at 43–49°C / 110–120°F to compensate — significantly warmer than the DDT — so the dough reaches the correct temperature after the cooling effect of the vacuum.

No poolish. This variation is a direct dough — no preferment. The polydextrose and vegetable shortening are the modernist additions that improve crust texture and crumb structure without fermentation complexity.

Polydextrose: A soluble fiber that adds a subtle sweetness and aids crust color and crispness. Do not substitute.

Bulk ferment is shorter than the standard recipe — 1¼ hours total with only 2 folds (vs. 1½ hours with 3 folds). The vacuum mixing achieves sufficient gluten development to allow this abbreviated schedule.

Crispy crust requires steam. See baking table below. All shapes bake at 245°C / 470°F.

Storage: Consume within 1 day, or freeze for up to 2 months.

Modernist Hamburger Buns

Modernist Hamburger Buns
Inspired by Heston Blumenthal

Yield 10 buns · ~100 g each
Active time ~9 minutes mixing
Total time ~4 hours 14 minutes
DDT 24–26°C / 75–78°F
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 6
Start the biga 12 hours ahead — it ferments overnight and is used in the dough the next day.

Osmotolerant yeast: Required for both the biga and the dough. High sugar content inhibits regular yeast; osmotolerant yeast is formulated to perform in enriched doughs. SAF Gold is the most widely available brand.

L-cysteine: A dough relaxer that reduces elasticity, making the dough easier to roll thin without springing back — key to the Blumenthal technique. If unavailable substitute 0.08% kiwi juice (by flour weight, ~0.4 g) or 0.001% glutathione.

Egg yolks only (not whole eggs): Yolks add richness and a deep golden color without the extra water of whites.

Vegetable shortening (not butter): Produces a more tender, pillowy crumb than butter — characteristic of this style.

Doneness: Internal temperature 90–93°C / 195–200°F. Baking times vary — always check with a thermometer.

Storage: Consume within 1 day (shorter than the standard bun), or freeze for up to 2 months.

Modernist Thin-Crust Pizza

Modernist Thin-Crust Pizza

Yield 2 pizzas · 40 cm / 16 in + ~25 g dough extra
Dough active time ~15 min
Total time ~3¾ hours (or overnight cold proof)
Modernist Cuisine · Kitchen Manual Vol. 4
Cold proof strongly recommended: After room-temperature proof, transfer dough to the refrigerator and cold-proof for 1 day at 4°C / 39°F. This produces a noticeably crispier crust than baking the same day. Remove dough from refrigerator 1½–2 hours before shaping.

Poolish: Mix 12–16 hours ahead. The 0.02 g yeast is minuscule — approximate by measuring ⅙ tsp yeast and dividing into 6 equal parts; use one part for the poolish.

Polydextrose: Added with the flour. Improves crust crispness and color. Do not substitute.

Dough relaxer (optional): Reduces gluten elasticity for easier rolling and stretching. Add to water or flour as indicated in the table below. Choose one option only.

Salt is added after low gluten development — not at the start. This is intentional: salt tightens gluten and slows hydration. Adding it early makes a stiff, uneven dough.

Home oven: 40 cm / 16 in is the maximum size that fits. Preheat a baking stone or steel at highest temperature for at least 45–60 minutes before baking.
